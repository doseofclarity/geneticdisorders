Basics of Genetics

Human genome – humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). 22 pairs of autosomes, 1 pair of sex chromosomes (XX female, XY male).

🔬 Key Definitions
Gene – basic unit of heredity. Allele – variant form of a gene. Genotype – genetic makeup. Phenotype – observable characteristics.
🧬 Chromosome abnormalities
Numerical (aneuploidy – extra/missing chromosome) or structural (deletions, duplications, translocations, inversions).
Key concept: Genetics plays a role in nearly every disease. Nurses must understand inheritance patterns to provide risk assessment and counseling.

Single‑Gene Disorders

🧠 Huntington Disease
Autosomal dominant; CAG repeat expansion on chromosome 4; onset 30–50 years: chorea, dementia.
💪 Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy
X‑linked recessive; dystrophin gene deletion; progressive weakness, wheelchair by adolescence.
🩸 Hemophilia A
X‑linked recessive; factor VIII deficiency; bleeding episodes, hemarthrosis.
🌬️ Cystic Fibrosis
Autosomal recessive; CFTR gene mutation; thick mucus → lung disease, pancreatic insufficiency.
🍼 Phenylketonuria (PKU)
Autosomal recessive; PAH deficiency; intellectual disability if untreated; managed with dietary restriction.
🧬 Fragile X Syndrome
X‑linked dominant (CGG expansion >200); most common inherited intellectual disability; large ears, macroorchidism.

Genetic Testing & Diagnostics

  • Karyotyping: Visualizes chromosomes; detects aneuploidy and large structural changes.
  • FISH (Fluorescence in situ hybridization): Rapid detection of specific chromosome abnormalities (e.g., trisomy 13, 18, 21).
  • Chromosomal Microarray (CMA): Higher resolution; detects microdeletions/duplications not visible on karyotype.
  • Prenatal diagnosis: Chorionic villus sampling (10–13 weeks), amniocentesis (15–20 weeks), NIPT (cell‑free fetal DNA).
  • Newborn screening: Bloodspot for PKU, congenital hypothyroidism, cystic fibrosis, sickle cell disease.
Note: Informed consent and genetic counseling are essential before and after testing.

Genetic Counseling & Nursing Role

  • Purpose: Provide information, assess risk, support decision‑making, and facilitate adaptation.
  • Key principles: Non‑directiveness, informed consent, confidentiality, respect for autonomy.
  • Nursing role: Take family history (≥3 generations), identify red flags, coordinate testing, offer emotional support, refer to genetics professionals.
  • Indications for referral: Advanced maternal age, previous child with anomaly, multiple miscarriages, consanguinity, family history of genetic disorder.
Nursing mnemonic (G.E.N.E.): G – Gather family history, E – Educate about inheritance, N – Navigate testing options, E – Empower and support.
